An Uncompromising Appeal to Female Rage: Why I Loved ‘A Promising Young Woman’

Posted by Avery Haly-Lock on February 26, 2021
Trigger warning: violence, sexual assult

"Rage - Goddess, sing the rage of Peleus' son Achilles, murderous, doomed, that cost the Achaeans countless losses.”

“Rage” is the first word in Homer’s Iliad, introducing the central theme of his work: the rage of Achilles. Rage and the desire for revenge are fundamental to the human experience, reflected in their prominence as themes in literature and art throughout the ages.

However, the fundamentally human emotion of rage is often afforded exclusively to men. There is no place for female anger in the normative, socially acceptable feminine ideal: the passive, helpless, forgiving - not angry, woman. Angry women are “offputting”, “unladylike” and “shrill”. Women are taught that displays of outrage and anger are irrational reactions, especially regarding political issues (and usually to matters that should in fact warrant anger.) In women, anger and emotion are irrational, corrupting of reason. In men they are justified, natural, and heroic. They are human.

Exhibit A: White Male Rage

Need evidence? Take for example the Kavanaugh trial. Would it be acceptable for anyone but a white man to have a temper tantrum in front of the U.S. supreme court? How would this display of emotions have gone over if the nominee was a woman? I can only imagine: “I guess it’s that time of the month.” “Typical woman.” “It’s the hormones”.

Images of the "hormonal woman" the "angry bitch" and the "man-hating feminist" all serve as examples of this fact. This is true also for minorities and people of color (anger for black men is not only unacceptable but dangerous). The scrutiny around female anger denies women a fundamental experience of humanness and personhood. Anger is reserved only for subjects, the protagonist, of which women cannot themselves be. After all, the main character in literature, art, politics, and all of (his)tory is male. Humanity is male, and therefore rage is male. This is what Simone de Beauvoir describes when she claims that man is the “subject”, the “self”, and woman the “other” the lesser, secondary sex (8). The denial of female rage is a denial of womens’ very humanness.

The culture that deems female rage unacceptable is also reflected in society’s treatment of rape victims. The "good" rape victim grieves quietly and discreetly, and does not give in to rage. The issue is all too often swept under the rug in college campuses, workplaces, and households. Shame and silence keep the issue of rape out of public view - rage is not conducive to this culture looking the other way.

This is all to say, A Promising Young Woman forces this issue into the spotlight. It highlights the unpleasant realities of rape culture that men like Kavanaugh would rather not have talked about. Promising Young Woman reclaims female rage, and subsequently reclaims womens’ position as the subject.

Female anger is far from irrational under the circumstances women face - discrimination, violence, double standards, paternalism - it is the very definition of rational. It is only from a position of privilege that men can preach a stance of “rational” neutral detachment, arguing that we should separate our emotions from political matters, that we should “just calm down”. Men never have to worry about walking alone at night, keeping an eye on their drink at the bar, or wondering if their clothes might make it seem like they’re “asking for it”. A Promising Young Woman embraces anger as a rightful, rational, and human response to these injustices.

The film is not directed at a male audience. The point of A Promising Young Woman is not to educate male viewers against rape culture. It does no hand-holding or makes any attempt to compassionately help men understand. It’s meant as an appeal to women, a call to ignite the rageful instinct that we have been taught to stamp out. It is an appeal to emotion and anger, the basest of human instincts that women have been denied. Cassie’s anger is the rage of Achilles, lashing out in a slow-burning, calculated revenge against the wrongdoings the protagonist has faced. No matter how immoral and unethical the actions of Cassie (the protagonist) may be, viewers, especially women, are forced to empathize and connect with her rage as a shared human experience, as well as a catharsis embracing the rageful instinct which women have been excluded from.

The film’s character Ryan, Cassie’s initial love interest who she discovers to have been a bystander to Nina’s assault, also serves a point about “nice guys”. Every guy is a “nice guy”. Brock Turner, Harvey Weinstein, Brett Kavanaugh (etc) were all “nice guys”. Even if they do not actively participate in sexual assault, “nice guys” are the bystanders, family members, and friends of these men, refusing to speak out against sexual assault. As long as the culture of looking the other way and systematic silencing of sexual assault survivors continues, there are no innocent men. Because the roots of the system, the criminal justice, legal, and cultural institutions that facilitate and cover-up sexual assault are too widespread and pervasive, no man can claim that he has no role in rape culture, because rape culture is everywhere. Cassie’s rage and unethical actions are the result of such a deeply corrupt system, a system that forces her to take justice into her own hands.
